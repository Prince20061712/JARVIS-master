Newton's Laws of Motion
Chapter 1: The Basics of Dynamics

1. Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)
Definition: An object will remain at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force.
Real-world Example: A book on a table stops moving only because friction acts on it. Without friction, it would slide forever.
Formula: F_net = 0 implies a = 0.

2. Newton's Second Law (Law of Acceleration)
Definition: The acceleration of an object as produced by a net force is directly proportional to the magnitude of the net force, in the same direction as the net force, and inversely proportional to the mass of the object.
Formula: F = ma
Where:
F = Net Force (Newtons, N)
m = Mass (kg)
a = Acceleration (m/s^2)

Derivation of F=ma:
From momentum (p = mv):
F = dp/dt
F = d(mv)/dt
If mass is constant:
F = m(dv/dt)
Since a = dv/dt,
F = ma.

3. Newton's Third Law
Definition: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.
Example: Walking on the ground - you push back, ground pushes forward.
